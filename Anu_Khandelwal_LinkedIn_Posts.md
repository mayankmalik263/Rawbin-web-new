### 3. 2026-06-01 — 🟢 Rawbin

**Topic:** Household food waste is the most expensive organic waste stream to collect.  
**Type:** post | **Likes:** 35 | **Comments:** 0 | **Shares:** 1  
**Post link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/akhandelwal25_household-food-waste-is-the-most-expensive-activity-7467209774154162176-mXsx  

**Description:**

> Household food waste is the most expensive organic waste stream to collect.
>
> I was recently at CEEW's discussion on unlocking India's organic waste circular economy, and one insight really stayed with me:
>
> India doesn't have a waste technology problem, we rather have:
> 1. feedstock quality problem, due to poor segregation at source
> 2. logistics problem, due to collection inefficienies and high transportation costs
>
> and these are primarly the main barriers why we are not able to scale up bulk composting technologies, biomethanation etc.
>
> During the event, we also discussed that how different technology works for different space. For eg: Biomethanation works for hotels, hostels, canteens, large apartment complexes etc. where waste generation amount is greater than 1 TPD or more. The collection is elimimated there and Biogas can be used onsite. Similarly bulk composting plants works for leaf litters, public parks etc. 
>
> You see we are not just solving waste processing here, we are also reducing transportion cost while keeping feedstock quality optimal.
>
> However, there is one space where the huge gap remains and which contributes to 50% of the problem: Indian households. They generate small amounts of waste 0.2 - 1.2 kg per day, plus they are highly distributed. So, transportation cost exceeds the material value. 
>
> So, whenever someone suggests me why don't you build a bigger Rawbin for hotels or large complexes - I say No, that's not actually the problem. We are here for households, smaller spaces - small quantities of food waste, spread across billions of homes, where segregation is not maintained and transportation is expensive.

**Description:**

> Technology alone cannot fix waste without behavioral change. 
>
> I still spend hours explaining this when someone says, “Why don’t you just build community composting systems?”
>
> The truth is - we don’t lack technology. There are many efficient community composters already. What fails is implementation.
>
> The real problem isn’t technical capability.
> It’s the lack of civic consciousness around waste.
>
> No matter how advanced a machine is, if households don’t segregate waste, the machine will eventually break down.
>
> That’s exactly why we built Rawbin - to drive behavioural change at the household level. We’re continuously working to make it affordable so more people can adopt it.
>
> When people start composting themselves, they don’t just reduce food waste. They become more mindful of dry waste too. They start respecting public spaces. These long-term impacts aren’t loud or instant - they grow quietly. We’ve already seen this with our early users (we call them Rawbinhoods 😄).
>
> Can’t wait to see this mindset spread across India and hopefully make India the first country where composting food waste becomes a household habit. 🌱

>
> Most solutions try to fix this from the supply side: farmers’ markets, F2C startups. Important, yes - but not enough. Research shows that availability alone doesn’t change behavior. Consumers rarely switch unless convenience and motivation are built in.
>
> *********This is where Rawbin flips the equation.***********
>
> By enabling households to compost their own food waste, Rawbin re-establishes the lost connection between consumption and cultivation. Rawbin directly creates a need for organic produce. This contrasts with the fragmented model where farmers struggle to find steady buyers for their indigenous seeds or organic crops. Instead of just providing a market for organic products, Rawbin generates organic produce demand intrinsically linked to everyday citizen behavior. 
>
> Smallholder farmers often struggle to sell through mandis due to competitive pricing from large traders, high commissions, and limited negotiation power. Through Rawbin’s ecosystem, we create direct, local demand - linking households that compost with nearby farmers and FPOs who practice sustainable growing. This shortens the value chain and improves farmer margins.

>  2️⃣ FW2 – Food preparation waste, including spoiled items
>  3️⃣ FW3 – Post-consumption remnants
>
> If a similar stratified study were conducted across Indian cities - covering homes, markets, and institutions - we’d likely find FW2 dominating, closely followed by FW3, which is often ignored and the hardest to recover due to poor segregation.
>
> 💡 Why FW3 is the Most Urgent to Address 
> 🔻 Low recovery rate: Unlike FW1 (surplus food) and FW2 (post-harvest retail/processing), FW3 is rarely recovered - most ends up mixed in municipal solid waste and becomes unusable for resource recovery. 
> 🔻 Immediate, localized intervention possible: FW3 occurs at the household and institutional level, making it ideal for decentralized, at-source solutions like Rawbin. 
> 🔻 Most complex to manage at scale if delayed: Once post-consumer waste is mixed and contaminated, centralized systems can’t process it effectively. That’s why prevention or treatment at source is crucial.
>
> This makes a strong case for a phased intervention model: 
> 1. Short-term focus on FW3 through at-source solutions like household composting (e.g. Rawbin and similar solutions)
> 2. Medium-term development of food recovery and redistribution programs for surplus edible food (FW1)
> 3. Long-term infrastructure investment in biofertilizer systems to handle bulk FW2 from markets and institutions.

>
> Lakh material, also known as lac, is a resinous substance secreted by the female lac insect (Laccifer Lacca). **Topic:** Does segregation really solve the waste problem? Do we have the capacity, res...  
**Type:** post | **Likes:** 25 | **Comments:** 2 | **Shares:** 0  
**Post link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/akhandelwal25_does-segregation-really-solve-the-waste-problem-activity-7087796209104424960-XfK1  

**Description:**

> Does segregation really solve the waste problem? Do we have the capacity, resources, labor, and infrastructure required for waste processing if the entire nation were to begin segregating waste tomorrow?
>
> This problem has multiple facets:
>
> Infrastructure capacity:
> Presently, Bengaluru has seven wet waste processing plants with a combined capacity of 1,620 tonnes per day. However, only five of these plants are operational, receiving approximately 900 tonnes of waste per day. This accounts for just 55.5% of the total capacity, indicating the plants' inefficiency. Stench and leachate emitted by plants are the major hindrances to the plants running at total capacity.
>
> Environmental challenges:
> Ideally, waste processing plants should be situated in uninhabited regions, preferably on the outskirts of cities. However, many existing plants are located within cities, posing a significant threat to nearby populations. Moreover, some plants are so poorly managed that nobody knows what happens to the wet waste there.
>
> Technical complexity:
> Composting is a highly scientific process that demands an understanding of factors such as the ideal C:N ratio, moisture, and temperature at different phases to ensure a methane-free harvest. The composting pile's dimensions, determined by mathematical specifications, must generate optimal heat. Adequate management measures must also be in place to address odor and leachate issues. Successful waste processing requires technical know-how and some knowledge of microbiology. And if any critical component is missed then the entire plant fails. It's so much risky.
>
> In conclusion, the problem doesn't end with segregation, which calls for innovative decentralized solutions that empower people to process their own waste.
